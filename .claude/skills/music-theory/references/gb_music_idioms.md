# GB Music Idioms — How the Original Composers Used 4 Channels

Understanding how the original tracks are structured tells you what's
already there and where the gaps are that your arrangement can fill.

---

## The 4 GB Channels

### Pulse 1 — The Melody
- Carries the primary melody in almost every track
- Uses duty cycle changes (12.5%, 25%, 50%, 75%) for timbral variation
- Range: typically C4–C6
- **Your arrangement**: don't compete with this. Stay below it or add
  harmony, never a second melody at the same pitch.

### Pulse 2 — Counter-Melody / Harmony
- Secondary melodic line, often in 3rds or 6ths with pulse 1
- Sometimes plays arpeggiated chord tones
- Sometimes silent — creates opportunities for your added parts
- Range: typically C3–C5
- **Your arrangement**: when pulse 2 is active, your inner voices need
  to avoid parallels with it. When it's silent, you have more room.

### Wave — Bass / Secondary Voice
- Usually the bass line (arpeggiated root–5th patterns or walking bass)
- In some tracks, carries a secondary melody or chord pad
- Limited volume control (4 levels only on the GB)
- Range: typically C2–C4
- **Your arrangement**: bass reinforcement should double this at the
  octave or add complementary motion, not contradict it.

### Noise — Percussion
- Rhythmic patterns: hi-hat, snare approximations, cymbal crashes
- 19 noise "instruments" defined in the engine
- **Your arrangement**: align your rhythmic additions with the noise
  channel's pattern. Don't add cross-rhythms unless the original does.

## Common Channel Configurations

### "Full band" (most tracks)
All 4 channels active:
- Pulse 1: melody
- Pulse 2: harmony/counter-melody
- Wave: bass
- Noise: percussion

**Gap**: inner harmony between melody and bass is thin. One voice
doing double duty (melody + harmony). Your additions fill the middle.

### "Trio" (quieter tracks)
3 channels, noise silent:
- Pulse 1: melody
- Pulse 2: counter-melody or sustained note
- Wave: bass

**Gap**: more harmonic space to fill. Good candidate for a warm pad.

### "Duet" (very sparse tracks)
2 channels:
- Pulse 1: melody
- Wave: bass (or pulse 2: bass)

**Gap**: wide open for arrangement. Counter-melody + pad both possible.

## Texture Patterns by Game Area

### Town themes (Pallet, Cerulean, Celadon, etc.)
- Moderate tempo, major key
- Arpeggiated bass (wave), singing melody (pulse 1)
- Pulse 2 fills in harmony, often in 3rds with melody
- **Arrangement approach**: warm sustained pads, gentle bass reinforcement
- **Avoid**: too much activity, cross-rhythms, dense textures

### Route/road themes
- Upbeat, walking tempo
- More rhythmic bass, sometimes walking
- Pulse 2 more independent (counter-melody)
- **Arrangement approach**: can be slightly more active. Counter-melody
  opportunities. Light rhythmic comping is acceptable.

### Battle themes (wild, trainer, gym leader, champion)
- Fast tempo, minor or mixed mode
- Driving bass, rapid pulse melodies
- Noise very active (driving beat)
- **Arrangement approach**: rhythmic additions, brass-like stabs,
  driving bass reinforcement. Can use denser textures.
- **Avoid**: sustained pads that blur the rhythmic energy

### Dungeon/cave themes
- Slow or moderate, minor key
- Sparse texture, often echo-like effects
- **Arrangement approach**: minimal. Sparse reverb pads, widely spaced.
  Less is more — don't fill the atmospheric space.

### Lavender Town / creepy themes
- Minor key, chromatic elements
- Pulse channels often play dissonant intervals deliberately
- **Arrangement approach**: very restrained. Match the dissonance.
  Maybe one eerie pad. Don't "fix" intentional tension.

### Fanfares / jingles (evolution, level up, badge, healing)
- Short, triumphant, major key
- All channels active for maximum impact
- **Arrangement approach**: these are already dense. Light reinforcement
  at most. Full chord voicing for the climactic moment.

## Phrasing Conventions

### Phrase structure
Most GB tracks use 4-bar or 8-bar phrases, often in sentence or period
form (see music-theory references for definitions). Recognize phrase
boundaries so your arrangement breathes with the music.

### Repeats
GB music loops. The engine handles repeat points. Your arrangement
should be consistent across repeats — the "orchestrate once, duplicate"
principle from the plan means you only need to arrange unique sections.

### Rests and breaths
When the melody rests (pulse 1 silent for a beat or more), **your added
parts should also rest** or at least thin out. Don't fill silence — it's
part of the phrasing.

### Dynamics (volume envelopes)
GB tracks use volume envelopes for expression (crescendo via increasing
duty, decrescendo via envelope decay). Your arrangement should follow
the same arc — don't play fortissimo during a fadeout section.

## Key Signatures in Pokémon Yellow

Most Pokémon Yellow music uses a small set of keys:

| Key | Example tracks |
|-----|---------------|
| C major | Pokémon Center, some menus |
| G major | Pallet Town |
| D major | Route themes |
| F major | Some town themes |
| B♭ major | Various |
| A minor | Lavender Town, caves |
| D minor | Team Rocket, some battle |
| E minor | Dungeon themes |

The original GB composers favored keys that sit well on the limited
pitch range and where the arpeggiated bass patterns fall naturally.
